pH: pH denotes the acidity or alkalinity of water, crucial for taste, corrosion control, and disinfection efficacy. It's measured using precise pH meters or pH test strips. The WHO recommends a pH range of 6.5 to 8.5 for drinking water, ensuring it remains palatable and minimizes corrosion. Hardness: Water hardness, primarily from calcium and magnesium ions, influences scale formation and soap efficiency. Detection involves test kits or titration methods. Total Solids: Total solids encompass dissolved and suspended matter, impacting water clarity and taste. Evaporation followed by residue weighing determines total solids. Drinking water standards often stipulate total dissolved solids (TDS) below 500 mg/L. Chloramines: Chloramines, disinfectants formed by chlorine and ammonia, ensure microbial safety. Detection employs specialized kits with colorimetric reactions. Drinking water guidelines recommend chloramine levels below 4.0 mg/L to prevent taste issues and potential health concerns . Sulfate: Sulfate levels, indicative of geological or anthropogenic influences, are determined via ion chromatography or turbidimetric analysis. Drinking water standards prescribe sulfate concentrations below 250 mg/L to prevent adverse taste and potential laxative effects. Conductivity: Electrical conductivity reflects ion concentration, a key indicator of water purity. Measured using precision meters, acceptable conductivity for drinking water typically falls between 50 and 1500 ŒºS/cm , ensuring minimal dissolved solids and optimal taste. Organic Carbon: Organic carbon, derived from natural and anthropogenic sources, influences taste and promotes disinfection byproduct formation. TOC analysis or UV absorbance methods detect its presence. Drinking water standards often mandate organic carbon levels below 2 mg/L for optimal taste and safety. Trihalomethanes (THMs): THMs, disinfection byproducts formed from organic matter and chlorine, pose health risks at elevated levels. Detection involves sophisticated techniques such as gas chromatography. Drinking water guidelines typically limit total THMs to below 0.080 mg/L to mitigate health concerns . Turbidity: Turbidity, caused by suspended particles, impacts aesthetics and filtration efficiency. Turbidimeters or nephelometers quantify turbidity. Drinking water standards mandate turbidity levels below 1 NTU to ensure clarity and effective disinfection .
